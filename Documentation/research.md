# Research

A double pendulum is a mechanical system where two rods are linked together by a pivot such that the motion of one influences the other. For simplicity, the pendulum will be modeled under the following assumptions: Any friction or energy loss is neglected; both rods are massless, rigid and equal in length; both masses are single points; and the system is confined to two dimensions. Modeling under these assumptions, negligible differences in initial angles can lead to drastically different motions over time. This sensitivity to initial conditions is known as chaos. The double pendulum is one of the foundational chaotic systems due its relatively low variable count, making it easier to model and study compared to other, more complex, chaotic systems. Despite its simplicity at first glance, it is one of the most commonly studied systems that exhibits the complicated topic of chaos. 

Mathematically, chaos utilizes concepts in vector calculus, advanced differential calculus, and multi-variable calculus, all of which are not ordinarily studied. Conceptually, chaos is characterized by a high sensitivity to a system's initial conditions, resulting in unpredictable outputs. This report briefly explores the concepts in each branch, but shifts focus onto how states of a double pendulum can be computed and modeled. For example, we can visualize whether a system is chaotic by creating Poincaré sections (fundamentally, a slice of a high dimensional space) and simplified phase space diagrams. For a double pendulum, phase space diagrams show relationships between the two angles of the masses and their momenta. Unfortunately, this system is uninterpretable and impossible to visualize because it has four variables and is therefore four dimensional. To combat this, we use two variables in two dimensions instead. As a result of the system’s intrinsic chaos, these diagrams can be difficult to compute accurately. Furthermore, certain programming techniques and mathematics are required to counter modeling and computation problems such as  Python’s floating-point precision errors. Even though chaotic systems are hard to model, they're important because they are fixed into our everyday lives. We encounter many chaotic systems such as the weather or fluid dynamics. The more these are understood, the more technology can progress.

Because the derived equation of motion for a double pendulum is nonlinear, a numerical integration method is required. A numerical integrator is a computational method that approximates the integral of an ordinary differential equation to predict the location of the next state of a system over a given time step. There are many families of numerical integrators from explicit single-step to multi-step, each used for different physical systems. Because a double pendulum is a non-stiff system (changes consistently with time scale) that’s relatively simple (smooth, few differential equations), an explicit single-step integrator is most appropriate. 

The Runge Kutta family consists of a number of different explicit single-step integrators, from forward Euler (or RK1) to RK2 to RK4. With each increasing order of RK, the time per computation increases but the system remains more stable. Using Taylor Series, we can derive the global error for the forward Euler, RK2, and RK4 to determine that RK4 has the highest ordered global error (4th order), proving that RK4 would have the least divergence out of the 3 numerical integrators. However, RK4 also has the highest time per computation out of the 3 numeral integrators and it’s difficult to determine whether the trade-off of accuracy for time would be effective in modeling a double pendulum. This project, therefore, first attempts to model a double pendulum using its equations of motions through forward Euler, RK2, and RK4 to determine which numerical integrator has the best balance between accuracy and speed.

One way to determine the accuracy of our methods is by using the conservation of energy law. This law states that for any closed system, the total energy will not change. By plotting total energy vs. time, we can see any inaccuracies and visually determine which is the most accurate. Because the double pendulum is a closed system, the integrator that has the least energy divergence will be used for future calculations. 

By plotting the graphs for each method, we will be able to determine the numerical integrator with the best balance between accuracy. We will then be able to use this numerical integrator as the integrator of choice for modeling a double pendulum. The Lyapunov exponent is a metric for determining the chaos of the double pendulum. The Lyapunov exponent is a variable that measures the rate of convergence/divergence between two physical systems over time. The Lyapunov exponent is mathematically defined in the Derivation Folder.

In this case, the two physical systems will be two double pendulums separated by a difference of 10e^-8 degrees. We will use the RK4 to simulate both of the physical systems and compute the distance trajectory between the masses of the double pendulums over time. By plotting the logarithm of the distance trajectory over time and fitting the plot into a linear graph, we can compute the Lyapunov exponent by taking the slope of the regression line. If the Lyapunov exponent is near 0, the distances are not converging nor diverging over time and the system is instead considered as periodic. If the Lyapunov exponent is greater than 0, the distances between the masses are exponentially diverging over time and is considered chaotic. 

In addition, a Poincaré section can be plotted alongside computing the Lyapunov exponent to observe chaotic motion in a dynamic system. For a high dimensional space, four dimensional for example, a Poincaré section takes a lower dimensional slice and records where a moving point intersects it. In this case, the state of a double pendulum is represented in a four dimensional space, and the Poincaré section is in two dimensions for easy visualization. The Poincaré section is a useful graph that can be plotted with any subset of the system’s state variables. For this project, a slice at θ₂=0 and ω₂>0 was chosen to be mapped, making the resulting axis ω₁ against θ₁. The restraint for the velocity prevents double crossing, meaning the slice will not record two points per oscillation, therefore stopping redundancy. In other words, the slice only records when the point crosses the plane in one direction, not both. The Poincaré section was defined this way to visualize a portion of a system’s motion at a time rather than all of it all at once. There are a few different observable behaviors on a Poincaré section: quasiperiodic, resonance, and nonlinear resonance just to name a few. For simplicity, only quasiperiodic and chaotic will be included in our results. Quasiperiodic behavior appears as one or more points or smooth loops that can have irrational but stable frequency ratios. Chaotic behavior appears as many points that randomly fill a space rather than an organized configuration. In addition, Poincaré maps display the strange attractor, which demonstrates a system’s constraint to its dynamical system. For any Poincare map, the system must stay within the boundaries of the attractor. In summary, analyzing a Poincaré map can help us better visualize and characterize the chaotic behavior of a double pendulum. 

The initial-condition chaos map is the best way to visualize the chaos of all initial states of the double pendulum. Each pixel on this map represents an initial condition of the double pendulum with zero momentum. The color of the pixel generally corresponds with a system's chaotic behavior, with black representing no chaos and colored representing chaos. Unlike any other visuals, this map can show the evolution of many states at once and display them distinctly. Some notable behaviors are present in the graph: systems with low energy tend to be non chaotic while systems with high energy tend to be chaotic. High energy often means that an arm of the double pendulum will flip over and complete a full rotation after some period of time. Intuitively, one may assume that this flip is what gives the pendulum chaos, and all pendulums that flip are chaotic. However, there are exceptions to this ‘rule’. Some pendulums that have high enough energy to flip do not exhibit chaotic behavior. Similarly, some pendulums that do not flip exhibit chaotic behavior regardless. Another notable behavior is the presence of islands of stability. Specifically, they appear as tiny sections of black in a sea of color. This indicates that there are special cases where the pendulum is not chaotic while those with very similar starting conditions are. Some, but not all, of these islands contain double pendulums that do not flip. The islands of stability where the pendulum does flip are the exceptions talked about previously. This reveals an important idea: the reason for chaos in dynamic systems is not yet completely understood. Although we can make general rules for how it works and predict its behavior, we do not know specifically why it happens.
